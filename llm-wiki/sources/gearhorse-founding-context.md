## Origin Story

Gear Horse was conceived around a campfire on the Deschutes National Forest, Oregon — a site used by the founding group for ~6 years running. The name came from a story about a stranger at a trailhead with a horse carrying an implausible amount of camping gear. The concept emerged over beers (Deschutes IPA) and a bowl, with three product ideas crystallizing in a single evening and saved to a phone Notes app at 6% battery.

The founding insight: the camping gear market is dominated by brands manufacturing in Asia, leaving a gap for premium, American-made specialty accessories that solve real campfire problems.

## Products

### Product 1 — Trail Maul (Mini Splitting Maul)
- **Problem:** No camp-scale splitting maul exists at retail. Hatchets can't split rounds; full mauls are impractical to transport.
- **Specs:** ~2.5 lbs, drop-forged American steel, hickory or fiberglass handle
- **Retail price target:** $89–$109
- **Gross margin target:** 55–65%
- **Manufacturer path:** ThomasNet → drop-forge shops in PA, OH, TN (ex-Craftsman/Sears suppliers with excess capacity)
- **IP:** File provisional patent ~$320 via USPTO; convert to utility patent ~$5,000 by Year 2
- **Name origin:** Campfire brainstorm, "it rhymes and it works"

### Product 2 — Ember Lid (Folding Campfire Pizza Lid)
- **Problem:** No foldable cast-iron campfire dome exists. Foil crimped over a pan is the current sad workaround.
- **Specs:** Cast iron, hinged/foldable, ~12"×12"×1.5" folded, ~13" cooking surface, temperature probe window
- **Retail price target:** $74–$94
- **Gross margin target:** 50–60%
- **Manufacturer path:** US cast-iron foundries via ThomasNet ("gray iron casting manufacturer"); prototype castings first
- **IP:** File provisional patent ~$320 via USPTO
- **Name origin:** "You're cooking on embers, and it rhymes"

### Product 3 — Chair Armor (Camp Chair Covers)
- **Problem:** No US-made, model-specific chair covers exist at any price point. Premium chairs degrade from UV/moisture/storage.
- **Specs:** Waxed canvas or 500D Cordura, sewn in USA, drawstring + buckle closure, model-specific and universal fit
- **Retail price target:** $38–$54
- **Gross margin target:** 60–70%
- **Manufacturer path:** Cut-and-sew manufacturers in NC, SC, GA via ThomasNet ("cut and sew manufacturer USA")
- **Name origin:** Finger guns from the whole group = highest honor

**Phase 1 — Validate (Months 1–2, $0)**
- Build landing page (Carrd Pro Lite)
- Post organic problem-focused content on r/camping, r/bushcraft — no pitch, just the problem

## Exit Strategy (5-Year Target)

Target: acquisition by outdoor brand within 5 years.

### Likely Acquirers (identified)
Lodge, Benchmade, Duluth Trading, Stanley, Solo Stove, BioLite, Snow Peak

### Acquisition Revenue Milestones
- $500K ARR → gets noticed
- $1M–$2M ARR → gets meetings
- $3M–$5M ARR with 60%+ gross margins + clean IP → gets a real offer

### Key Revisions for Exit-Focused Plan (vs. Profit-Focused Plan)
1. **Corporate structure:** Form as S-Corp or C-Corp from day one; clean books in QuickBooks from month one
2. **Narrow brand story:** Own one lane — "American-made campfire cooking brand" or "American-made camp tools brand." Don't sprawl.
3. **IP portfolio:** Provisional patents Year 1, utility patents Year 2 (~$5,000 with patent attorney). IP removes acquirer fear of Chinese knockoffs post-acquisition.
4. **Recurring revenue:** Subscription that requires owning Gear Horse gear — e.g. quarterly cast-iron seasoning oil + fire-starter kit for Ember Lid owners, or "camp cook" recipe/spice pack. Small recurring revenue dramatically improves exit multiple.
5. **Brand metrics scorecard:** Track monthly: email list size, repeat purchase rate, AOV, CAC, NPS. Acquirers pay for audiences, not just products.
6. **Strategic capital in Year 2–3:** $50K–$150K friends-and-family or angel round. Clean equity structure (not loans).
7. **Get products in front of likely acquirers early** — become their customers or retail partners before pitching a sale.
8. **Valuation multiples:** 2–5x revenue is typical for a brand with strong identity and defensible margins.

## Subscriber & Community Retention Research

*(Research conducted 2026-04-14 across outdoor subscription and email marketing sources)*

### Key Finding
Subscription boxes alone don't build loyalty — **community does**. The subscription/email list is infrastructure. The brand narrative is the product.

### What Works for Outdoor Brands

**1. Capture subscriber context at signup**
Simple preference question at opt-in ("car camper, backpacker, or homesteader?") enables segmentation and radically improves email relevance. Outdoor email platforms like Omnisend report automated messages drive disproportionate revenue share vs. volume.

**2. Lead with story, not product**
Arc'teryx, YETI, The North Face all lead with adventure narratives. For Gear Horse: document the founding story, manufacturer sourcing, first campfire tests. This content is uncopiable and is the Year 1 content calendar.

**3. Ambassadors, not influencers**
Long-term relationships with 5–8 real campers/bushcrafters (5k–50k followers). Series > one-offs. Let the ambassador live a whole arc, not just a highlight reel.

**4. UGC as trust engine**
Branded hashtag (e.g. #MadeToSplit, #EmberLidCook). Feature customer adventures back to them in email. Social proof from peers converts better than brand claims.

**5. Email sequences that teach, not sell**
Ember Lid buyer → 4-email sequence: fire-building basics → campfire pizza recipe → cast iron care → gentle Trail Maul cross-sell. First three emails sell nothing.

**6. The right Gear Horse subscription model**
NOT a generic gear box (churn driver = irrelevance). Instead: **consumables subscription tied to product ownership** — quarterly seasoning oil + fire-starter kit for Ember Lid owners. Defensible because it requires owning the gear first.

**7. Subscription churn drivers to avoid**
- Irrelevant products for the subscriber's activity type
- Repeated product categories (Nomadik research: subscribers receive "too many knives")

### Retention One-Liner
> Sign people up with an honest founding story, segment them immediately by activity, send genuinely useful content between purchases, feature their real adventures back to them, and sell a consumables subscription that only makes sense if they already own your gear.
